E-Collar Basics

An e-collar (electronic collar, sometimes called a "remote collar") is a training tool with a receiver worn on the dog's neck and a handheld transmitter. Used well, it's a way to communicate at a distance; used badly, it's just a way to scare a dog. This page covers how it works, what to buy, and how to start.

How it works

The transmitter (remote) sends a signal to the receiver (collar), which delivers a static stimulation, a vibration, or a beep, depending on the mode you choose. Reputable modern collars run on a very wide range of levels — most dogs train on a level so low that you can barely feel it on your own hand.

Key terms

Stimulation level
The intensity of the signal, usually on a numbered dial from 1 up to 100 or more. The goal is a level your dog notices, not one that startles them.
Tap
A very short, single pulse of stimulation, tapped once like a light shoulder-tap to get attention.
Continuous
Stimulation that runs as long as you hold the button, generally reserved for more advanced, specific applications.
Range
How far the transmitter can reliably reach the receiver, anywhere from a few hundred feet to a mile or more, depending on the model.

Where to buy

Look for a collar with a documented, gradual stimulation range and a solid warranty. I've been happy with gear from the E-Collar Technologies website, which also publishes plain-language guides on fitting and using their units.

Getting started safely

  1. Fit the collar snugly enough that the contact points touch skin, but you can still slide two fingers underneath.
  2. Find your dog's working level: the lowest setting they visibly notice, tested with the collar off their neck first.
  3. Pair the sensation with a command your dog already knows well, like a solid "here."
  4. Keep first sessions under ten minutes, in a low-distraction space like your living room.
  5. Only move outdoors or add distractions once the indoor response is fast and calm.
